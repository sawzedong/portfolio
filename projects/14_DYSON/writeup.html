Introduction to Dyson Work Attachment

As part of the Engineering and Tech Programme Scholarship (ETPS), I was given the opportunity to participate in a short work attachment during the June or December holidays. I applied and successfully entered into the Dyson five-day work attachment.

My Experience

The programme began with a visit to Dyson Global Headquarters, for an introduction to Dyson, its products, and on how it utilises packaging design. We were guided by two Dyson engineers to better understand the importance of packaging, considering functional, aesthetic, and experiential aspects.

On Day 2, we visited Dyson Global Development Campus in Johor, Malaysia. There, we learnt more about the process from conceptualisation to production. We saw various 2D and 3D tools used to conceptualise the packaging as well as tests done to ensure the robustness of the final design.

Finally, the last three days were for us to work on our own design. We were challenged to design a new packaging for the Dyson Supersonic Nural™ hair dryer with the added context of considering SG59. Throughout these days, we were at the Singapore University of Technology and Design (SUTD) labs, whereby we were guided through 2D and 3D Computer-Aided Design and using the tools available such as laser cutters and 3D printers.

Our Product

Our product was a durian-shaped case, whereby it was meant as a memento piece. We designed it as such to capture a iconic aspect of Singapore culture. This was enclosed in a box which is meant to unfold by itself upon opening, giving it a grand effect of opening and "cracking" the durain.

We achieved this through using 3D printing. The spikes were also strategically placed such that the case was still stable even when placed down. For the inside, we cut out acrylic and cardboard for the hairdryer tray and poles for easy coiling of the wire. While the colours were not quite perfect due to time constraints, we managed to mostly build out what we had in mind.

Reflections

It was inspiring to learn about how James Dyson started the business over a 5127 prototype struggle to make a vacuum cleaner. This helped me to understand its background and hence the importance of the pursuit to always continue innovating and prototyping.

For the programme itself, seeing this emphasised how many different considerations are often crucial to delivering solutions. Even with good products and ideas, the last-mile of minute details such as packaging can play a significant role in how people feel when using the product. Seeing the amount of effort dedicated to something that I may often overlook also highlighted the importance of good design; "Good design, when it's done well, it becomes invisible."

Disclaimer: As these works were done years in the past, I do not necessarily believe these are my best works and I recognise their limitations. However, they remain available for reference and also as a way to chart my growth!
