Introduction to Research@YDSP (R@YDSP)

Under the Young Defence Scientists Programme (YDSP), DSTA and DSO conduct R@YDSP internships for students to participate in research to solve real-life defence problems. It consisted of a 3-month long online phase where there was heavy focus on literature review and report writing, and a 1-month long on-site internship for data collection and modelling.

My Research Journey

I took part in R@YDSP as part of a team of four and one mentor from DSTA. I researched on the topic of "Optimising Counter-Unmanned Aerial Systems Efforts for National Security". During the literature review, I was introduced to the problem that Unmanned Aerial Systems (UAS) can pose, exacerbated by their ease of access due to low cost. This provided me with the chance to also study Counter-UAS (C-UAS) methods that are employed.

During the on-site internship, I was able to learn about the different type of Line-of-Sight and Radio Frequency propagation models, adapting the various models for their respective terrains. We also researched on Global Navigation Satellite System and its effective jamming mechanisms.Through running programming simulations and calculations, we were able to determine the range of effectiveness of certain C-UAS deployments.

We were also given the opportunity to visit the Civial Aviation Authority of Singapore (CAAS) to see how they practically employ such methods to watch for UAS threats. Overall, by combining our calculations with each type of defence's unique capabilities, we applied this to a real world scenario. We analysed the key installation and deduced the effective ranges to see how such defences could be optimally placed.

Reflections and Conclusion of the Research

This research was meaningful as it was one of my first internships and I felt highly supported yet capable of synthesising my own ideas. This research continued to develop my research process from start to finish, including the literature review, experimentation, and report writing. There was a strong focus on good scientific communication while we were given the freedom to tackle the problem from our own point of view. My team was also close-knit and bonded which made it even more enjoyable.

This culminated in a YDSP Congress in April 2023 when I had the chance to present my poster to interested participants. Overall, I am glad I had this research opportunity to hone both my soft and hard skills while also being my first experience in an office-like setting.
